\subsection*{B. Specific comments (by section/page; numbered)}

\noindent\textbf{Comments as submitted (copy here):}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item p. 1, lines 18–27 (Abstract – Methods): The description of the Python‑based OR‑Tools CP‑SAT implementation is very high level and does not specify the objective function, relative weights of the soft constraints (fairness, spacing, didactic protection, non‑call requests), or key solver settings (e.g., time limits, optimality gap criteria). This makes it difficult for readers to understand how the solver actually balances competing priorities or to reproduce the approach in another setting.
    \item p. 1, lines 18–27 (Abstract – Methods): Given that the solver design is central to the innovation,
    consider briefly indicating whether the code or a deidentified version of the scheduling tool is available (e.g., via a public repository) and what inputs it expects (resident list, rotations, PTO, holidays). Without this information, the claim that the approach “can be adopted by other programs” is harder to evaluate
    \item p. 2, lines 9–22 (Methods – first paragraph under “Methods”): The statement that “each resident‑day pairing was represented as a binary decision variable” and that the solver “minimized weighted penalties for soft preferences” remains somewhat abstract; readers are not told what those penalties look like mathematically or how large the weights are relative to each other. Providing either a compact objective function (in an appendix) or a table listing the main soft constraints, their penalty expressions, and chosen weights would greatly improve transparency
\end{enumerate}
