  Generative Artificial Intelligence Use in Pediatric Residency Personal Statements
                                                --Manuscript Draft--

Manuscript Number:                    JGME-D-26-00298

Full Title:                           Generative Artificial Intelligence Use in Pediatric Residency Personal Statements

Short Title:

Article Type:                         Original Research

Keywords:                             artificial intelligence, personal statements, pediatric residency

Abstract:                             Background

                                      Use of artificial intelligence (AI, i.e. ChatGPT) has caused concern about personal
                                      statement authenticity in residency applications. This may threaten holistic review and
                                      ability to assess applicant characteristics. Humans struggle to determine which
                                      personal statements are AI versus human-generated.

                                      Objective

                                      To assess the frequency of AI use and percent of personal statement text generated by
                                      AI among pediatric residency applications using 2 AI detection platforms.

                                      Methods

                                      Retrospective cross-sectional study of de-identified residency applications from a
                                      single pediatric program between 2022-2024. Demographic information and interview
                                      status were abstracted. Personal statements were entered into two AI detectors
                                      (Quillbot, ZeroGPT) which determined AI% generated text. Outcomes were compared
                                      across applicant characteristics and time.

                                      Results

                                      3521 applications were assessed (1255 in 2022, 1139 in 2023, 1157 in 2024). The
                                      correlation between the AI% detected in each platform was .68 (95% CI .66, .7). Mean
                                      AI% detection across all years was 11.7% (SD 26.3) in Quillbot and 13% (SD 20.5) in
                                      ZeroGPT. A significant increase in mean AI% was detected in both platforms between
                                      2022 and 2024 (p<.001). Significant differences in AI% were found by self-identity and
                                      medical school country (p<.001). In adjusted analysis, no statistically significant
                                      difference existed in AI% detection among applicants invited to interview and those
                                      who were not invited (mean difference .5, 95% CI -1.8, 2.8).

                                      Conclusion

                                      AI use in pediatric residency personal statements is increasing. Significant variability in
                                      AI detection exists across platforms, questioning their accuracy. Stakeholders should
                                      consider how to integrate and monitor AI use among applicants.

 2
 3
 4             The emerging use of generative artificial intelligence (AI) by the general public is raising
 5
 6
 7   concerns about personal statement authenticity in residency applications. Personal statements
 8
 9   have been a longstanding and integral part of residency applications, allowing applicants to share
10
11
12   and expand on their innate personal characteristics and express their “goodness of fit” for a
13
14   particular specialty or program.1-3 Prior to AI, plagiarism and fabrication in personal statements
15
16
17   were already of concern, and the increasingly widespread use of AI may contribute further to
18
19   concerns regarding authenticity. Currently, the Electronic Residency Application System
20
21   (ERAS) advises: “The use of AI tools is acceptable for brainstorming, proofreading, or editing
22
23
24   the personal statement, but the final submission should represent your own work.5”
25
26             Despite program directors’ (PDs) debate over the value of personal statements in the
27
28
29   screening process for residency interviews, many PDs rank them highly in terms of importance.6-
30
31   10
          Historically, residency PDs have relied heavily on United States Medical Licensing Exam
32
33
34   (USMLE) scores when screening residency applications to make interview decisions.7,11 Yet,
35
36   with increasing numbers of medical schools transitioning from traditional grading schemes to
37
38
39
     pass/fail evaluations, and the USMLE Step 1 becoming pass/fail, it has become increasingly
40
41   difficult for residency PDs to rely on these objective measures as part of their applicant screening
42
43   process and is placing more emphasis on other applicant characteristics, such as personal
44
45
46   statements. PDs who weigh personal statements highly in the recruitment process believe they
47
48   offer meaningful insight into applicants’ communication skills, allow applicants to highlight
49
50
51   unique attributes, and articulate how their values align with a program’s mission and vision.2,8,12
52
53   PDs agree that personal statements are more meaningful when they share insight into the
54
55
56
     decision to pursue a specific specialty training, or elaborate upon their vision for potential career
57
58   plans after training.2,12

 2
 3
 4          Long before the advent of AI tools, some applicants used paid services to strengthen their
 5
 6
 7   residency applications, such as hiring contractors to review or revise personal statements.13 The
 8
 9   emergence of publicly available generative AI services (i.e. ChatGPT) represents a natural
10
11
12   extension of this longstanding practice and offers a lower-cost and more accessible alternative to
13
14   traditional editing services. Since the public release of ChatGPT-4 in 2022, there has been an
15
16
17   uptick in use of generative AI in assisting with the creation of personal statements.14,15 Several
18
19   studies across multiple specialties have demonstrated that generative AI models can create high
20
21   quality personal statements that are often indistinguishable by expert faculty reviewers from
22
23
24   human written statements.16-20 The ability to distinguish between the two types of content is
25
26   increasingly difficult as more detailed and advanced prompts with specific applicant details are
27
28
29   used to craft AI-generated statements.21
30
31          In addition to human review, numerous AI-detection tools have been created in response
32
33
34   to the growing use of generative AI in narrative writing as a way to flag possible AI-generated
35
36   content given the growing concern about authenticity and ethical use of AI. Use of AI-detection
37
38
39
     tools on personal statements from non-pediatric residencies written before and after 2022 has
40
41   revealed a marked increase in the proportion flagged as AI-generated after 2022.14,22
42
43          In this study, we aimed to assess the percent of AI-generated text as determined by two
44
45
46   AI detection platforms in pediatric residency applicant personal statements and its frequency of
47
48   use between 2022 and 2024. We hypothesized that there would be an increase in AI detection
49
50
51   rates in pediatric residency personal statements over this timeframe as ChatGPT became
52
53   increasingly available with widespread use among the general population.

57
58
59

 2
 3
 4          This was a retrospective, cross-sectional study at a single tertiary care academic center in
 5
 6
 7   New England. Applicant rosters and materials including personal statements were downloaded
 8
 9   from the Electronic Residency Application System (ERAS) archives for those applying in 2022
10
11
12   through 2024. Applicant characteristics abstracted from the rosters included gender, self-identity,
13
14   medical school country of origin, and interview status. Applicants were excluded if no personal
15
16
17   statement was present in their application materials. Each personal statement was copied directly
18
19   from the application file and entered into two AI detection platforms: Quillbot (Quillbot.com)
20
21   and ZeroGPT (ZeroGPT.com). These platforms were chosen as they are both free, open-access
22
23
24   platforms and have been used in prior studies with good accuracy.21, 24 We also used ChatGPT to
25
26   create an AI generated personal statement to test these platforms; both platforms reported 100%
27
28
29   of the text was AI generated. ChatGPT (Open AI Inc, San Francisco, CA) was released to the
30
31   public for commercial use on November 30, 2022, and hence we presumed that applicants would
32
33
34   not have had access to this technology for the 2022 application cycle. The Yale School of
35
36   Medicine Institutional Review Board deemed this study exempt from informed consent.
37
38
39
            Quillbot indicates the percent of text that is likely AI, the percent that is AI generated, the
40
41   percent that is AI generated and AI refined, the percent that is human generated and AI refined,
42
43   and the percent that is human generated (Figure 1a). The percent of text that is likely AI is the
44
45
46   summative value of the AI generated and AI generated and AI refined percentages. ZeroGPT
47
48   provides only the percentage of text likely generated by AI (Figure 1b). The output from both AI
49
50
51   detectors was manually transcribed onto the data abstraction forms. To ensure consistent results,
52
53   a sampling of personal statements was re-run through both detectors.
54

            Descriptive analyses were performed, including frequencies with percentages, means
57
58   with standard deviation (SD), and medians with interquartile ranges (IQR). Mean AI detection

     Results:
22
23
24          There were a total of 3521 applicant files available for the study period. Personal
25
26   statements were missing in 3 applications and were excluded from further analysis. Applicant
27
28
29   characteristics and interview status for the sample and for each year of the study are presented in
30
31   Table 1. Mean AI detection across all years was 11.7% (SD 26.3) in Quillbot at 13% (SD 20.5)
32

34   in ZeroGPT. Mean AI detection in each platform and by applicant characteristics is presented in
35
36   Table 2. The correlation between the total AI detected in each platform was .68 (95% CI .66, .7).
37

     There was a significant increase in mean AI detection in both platforms between 2022 and 2024
40
41   (Table 2). The proportion of applicants with any AI use also increased by year (p<.001, Figure
42
43   2). After adjusting for other applicant characteristics (eg. gender, self-identity, medical school
44

47

49
50
51   difference 0.5, 95% CI -1.8, 2.8).
52
53   Discussion:
54
55
56          Our study demonstrated an increase in AI generated content in pediatric residency
57
58
59   personal statements over the timeframe of 2022-2024 across both AI detection platforms. We
60
61
62
63
64                                                                                                        6
65
 1
 2
 3
 4   also found variability across AI detectors in how they classify text as AI- versus human-
 5
 6
 7   generated, as reflected in the moderate correlation between the two platforms. Both detectors
 8
 9   produced false positives, as labeled AI-generated content in personal statements from 2022 was
10
11
12   written before the public release of ChatGPT. These findings raise concern about the reliability
13
14   and accuracy of AI detectors, similar to what previous studies have shown.15, 23-25
15
16
17           While AI detection tools have become increasingly available, there is varying data on
18
19
20   their reliability and accuracy in detecting AI versus human generated content. Some studies
21
22   report numbers as high as 90% accuracy, while others are much lower (below 80% accuracy)
23
24
25   across multiple detectors.23-26 In addition, AI detectors often misclassify non-native English
26
27   writing as AI-generated, creating the potential for bias.27 These challenges highlight the need to
28
29
     better understand how AI detection tools perform in contexts that have not yet been studied
30
31
32   extensively, including pediatric residency applications.14,27
33
34
35          When reviewing AI-generated content use across demographics, we found that applicants
36
37
38   who attended non-U.S. medical schools had higher AI detection rates than those who attended
39
40   U.S. medical schools, as well as differences in AI-detection rates across self-identified
41
42
43   race/ethnicity categories. Notably, those applicants who self-identified as Middle Eastern or
44
45   “Other” had higher means of AI-detection, particularly in comparison to those applicants who
46
47
48   self-identified as White. This raises concern about AI detectors misclassifying written text by
49
50   non-native English speakers and supports the notion that detecting AI-generated content remains
51
52   difficult and that results from an AI-detector must be interpreted with caution.27 It is also
53
54
55   possible that non-native English speakers use AI more often to help optimize language, style, or
56
57   grammar in their personal statements.
58
59
60
61
62
63

 1
 2
 3
 4          Reality suggests that generative AI is here to stay, and its integration into everyday life
 5
 6
 7   and professional practice will continue to expand. When third and fourth year medical students
 8
 9   were surveyed about their intentions to use AI in the creation of their personal statements for
10
11
12   residency applications, more than half of the responders in the survey reported having already
13
14   used or were planning to use generative AI for assistance when writing their personal
15
16
17   statements.28 Our study data support these findings with some AI detection present in a large
18
19   proportion of residency applicants’ personal statements. In contrast, when plastic surgery faculty
20
21   were surveyed about the use of generative AI in residency personal statements, most believed it
22
23
24   to be unethical, with a minority supporting its use for editing personal statements or
25
26   brainstorming ideas. A very small minority supported its use in all aspects of creation and
27
28
29   writing of the personal statement.29
30
31
32          Having insight into which applicants used AI in creating their personal statement may
33
34
35   also introduce another level of bias to the residency selection process. Program directors have
36
37   raised concerns about an applicant’s work ethic if they knew that they used generative AI in the
38
39
40
     creation of their personal statement, which could negatively impact their candidacy.30,31 Without
41
42   having reliable AI detection, it may be dangerous to prematurely make a potentially false
43
44   assumption. We did not find a significant difference in interview status between applicants who
45
46
47   appeared to have AI-generated personal statements compared to those that were human written.
48
49   This may be related to our pediatric residency’s process of holistic review for applicants and
50
51
52   which factors play into offering an interview, in addition to previously discussed limitations
53
54   regarding the potential misclassification of AI versus human generated text by an AI detector as
55
56
57   well as the ability for AI models to generate high quality personal statements. Overall, other
58
59   studies to date have reported mixed findings: some show higher interview rates for ChatGPT-
60
61
62
63
64                                                                                                        8
65
 1
 2
 3
 4   generated statements (when reviewers are blinded to AI vs. human generated content), while
 5
 6
 7   others demonstrate that statements perceived as human-written by expert reviewers are more
 8
 9   likely to result in interview invitations.16,32
10
11
12
13           There are several limitations to our study. First, our study represents retrospective data
14
15   from a single academic center and medium-sized residency program in New England, and this
16
17
18   may limit generalizability, as applicant pools and selection criteria for pediatric residency may
19
20   vary across geographic regions and program sizes.
21
22
23
     Second, given the known variability in the accuracy of the two AI detectors used in this study, our
24
25   results may include both false positives and false negatives in terms of AI versus human-
26
27   generated content. Given that this was a retrospective study of ERAS applications, we could not
28
29
30   confirm whether or not applicants used AI in the creation of their personal statements, making AI
31
32   detector output an imperfect proxy for AI use. In addition, we also only used two AI detectors in
33
34
35   our analysis, both of which were free versions and use different algorithms in their models for
36
37   detecting AI generated content. Also, although ERAS applications indicate language fluency, this
38
39
40
     field changed over the three years of our study collection, precluding a more nuanced analysis of
41
42   preferred language and AI use. Lastly, AI familiarity, use, and sophistication rapidly grew
43
44   between 2022 and 2024; if AI detector technology did not evolve at a similar pace, then their
45
46
47   ability to distinguish AI from human-generated text may have changed over time.
48
49           Given the current limitations of AI detectors, residency PDs should approach AI using
50
51
52   detection cautiously. If residency PDs choose to use commercially available AI detectors as part
53
54   of their workflow in screening personal statements, they must be aware of the potential bias they
55
56
57   create without knowing with certainty how an applicant used (or did not use) AI in the creation of
58
59   their personal statement. Residency applicants’ use of AI in their personal statements makes it
60
61
62
63
64                                                                                                        9
65
 1
 2
 3
 4   increasingly difficult to differentiate between candidates during the screening process and poses
 5
 6
 7   significant challenges for consistent enforcement. It is possible that AI detector technology will
 8
 9   continue to improve with ongoing training and refinement similar to AI-generated technology,
10
11
12   and this will warrant ongoing surveillance. Future work should prioritize establishing standards
13
14   for ethical use around AI in residency applications as this is a technology that will continue to
15
16
17   advance and will be widely used by the general public.
18
19
20
21
     Conclusion:
22
23
24           Generative AI use in pediatric residency applications is increasing and will continue to
25
26   evolve as large language models advance and become integrated into daily life. Rather than
27
28
29   discouraging the use of generative AI outright, educators should work towards establishing best
30
31   practices that support applicants using AI responsibly, including disclosure of use. This
32
33
34   underscores the importance of creating clear guidance on its ethical and transparent use. Given
35
36   that current AI detectors remain unreliable and risk introducing bias into the residency selection
37
38
39
     process, PDs should use them cautiously.

              Dear Dr. Sullivan,


              Please accept our contribution to the literature entitled: Use of Generative Artificial Intelligence
              in Pediatric Residency Personal Statements. We believe it will make three important
              contributions to the current field of artificial intelligence use in medical education:

                  1. To our knowledge, this study is relatively novel in investigating the use of generative
                     artificial intelligence (AI) by pediatric residency applicants.

                  2. This manuscript supports the rising use of artificial intelligence use by pediatric
                     residency applicants and the challenge in detection of human-generated versus AI-
                     generated content in their personal statements.

                  3. This manuscript will provide further guidance for the considering the establishment of
                     best practices for responsible artificial intelligence use by pediatric residency applicants,
                     including disclosure.


              All authors participated in the conception, design, execution, and writing of the manuscript. The
              authors have no personal financial disclosures to report. This study was approved by the Yale
              School of Medicine IRB, which waived the need for informed consent.

              We wish to note that this manuscript is being submitted alongside a companion paper entitled
              “Growing Use of Artificial Intelligence in Pediatric Fellowship Personal Statements.” The two
              manuscripts report complementary findings yet address distinct research questions and have
              been prepared as separate submissions for focused audiences.
