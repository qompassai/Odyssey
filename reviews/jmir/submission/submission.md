General comments
=============
This manuscript addresses an important and underexplored aspect of degenerative cervical myelopathy (DCM)—the measurement of fatigue—which is particularly relevant given the forecasted increase in DCM prevalence due to aging populations and persistent challenges in care access that make accurate fatigue assessment paramount for effective care planning and rehabilitation strategies. The authors' patient-centered approach, involving people with lived DCM experience to evaluate 19 existing fatigue instruments, aligns with contemporary best practices in outcome measurement development. However, critical methodological limitations substantially weaken the paper's conclusions (see below comments). Given the import of this topic, it is imperative that these be addressed in order to ensure that the substantial effort from this international cohort delivers a meaningful impact on the greater understanding of DCM.
=============
Major comments
---------------------
1.The study's 9-person PwDCM panel provides valuable preliminary insights, but the manuscript would be substantially strengthened by validating findings against the larger, independent AO Spine RECODE-DCM minimum dataset already cited as reference 11 (Davies et al., PLOS Medicine 2024; N=113 PwDCM). Specifically, the authors should compare whether the four major themes identified by their panel—sleep disturbance, pain, mental health, and employment impact (Pages 11-12)—align with the core outcome domains designated by the 113 PwDCM in the Delphi consensus process. This triangulation would directly address concerns about sample size limitations and demonstrate convergent validity across independent patient samples. Adding a brief Results subsection (e.g., "External Validation") and corresponding Discussion paragraph would transform this acknowledged limitation into evidence of generalizability without requiring additional primary data collection.
2.The manuscript correctly identifies DCM as a condition with multifactorial fatigue etiology—including neurological impairment, psychosocial factors, and musculoskeletal dysfunction (Page 5; Figure 3, Page 24). However, critical gaps exist in both participant characterization and the evaluation framework. First, the Methods section (Pages 6-7) provides minimal demographic data for the 9 PwDCM raters (age, mJOA, gender, location) but does not report comorbidities, body habitus, treatment history, or current symptom severity—factors that would substantially influence how individuals perceive and rate fatigue measurement tools. For example, a PwDCM with significant obesity and sleep apnea may prioritize different scale features than one with isolated neurological symptoms. Second, the Discussion does not address how the recommended scales (MFIS-SCI, MAF, FACIT-F) account for—or fail to distinguish—fatigue attributable to primary DCM pathophysiology (cord compression, neurological dysfunction) versus secondary comorbidity contributions (obesity, poor nutrition, socioeconomic stressors, deconditioning). The authors should report comorbidity profiles of the 9 raters to establish sample representativeness, discuss whether these instruments require concurrent assessment of confounding factors (pain scales, sleep indices, mental health measures, BMI) to appropriately interpret fatigue scores, and explicitly address how researchers should attribute fatigue sources when using these tools in DCM populations with heterogeneous comorbidity profiles. This clarification is essential for appropriate clinical implementation and interpretation of results.
3. The Abstract and Conclusions state that MFIS-SCI, MAF, and FACIT-F "appear to be the most appropriate and validated instruments" for DCM, but this language overstates preliminary content validity assessment through patient perception of relevance, comprehensiveness, and clarity. The manuscript should more precisely characterize these as  preliminary data and explicitly note that other validity components (structural validity, responsiveness, minimal clinically important difference) remain unestablished. Additionally, given the 25-author international collaboration with direct RECODE-DCM involvement, the authors have a unique opportunity to identify which specific features of these three scales (recall period, multidimensional structure, inclusion of psychosocial items) drove their high ratings, then systematically compare whether these features align with scale selections in the published minimum dataset and their well-researched literature review. This would provide actionable guidance for researchers choosing between these three instruments rather than simply listing all three as equivalent options.

Minor comments
---------------------
The biopsychosocial model presented in Figure 3 provides a useful conceptual framework for understanding fatigue contributors in DCM, and the word cloud  effectively visualizes patient priorities. However, the manuscript would be strengthened by differentiating between primary contributory and secondary contributors Specifically, the authors should integrate the word cloud findings with the biopsychosocial model by indicating which factors patients emphasize most frequently and map these to the model's pathways. This would help researchers prioritize which concurrent assessments to include alongside fatigue scales when designing studies and provide guidance on which confounding variables require statistical control or stratification in DCM fatigue research.

Comments to Editor

I am concerned about the fact that there are more than twice the number of authors, and three times as many pages as
there are study participants that presents severely underpowered and unfocused findings. I recommend major
revisions requiring external validation against the minimum dataset or substantial reframing as explicitly preliminary
work with appropriately tempered conclusions. Without such changes, accepting this manuscript risks setting a
problematic precedent for publishing underpowered studies from large collaborative networks that fail to leverage
their own available resources for methodological strengthening.
